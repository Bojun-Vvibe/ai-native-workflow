# Worked example: report from `sample-session.jsonl`

The sample log captures one ~2-minute review mission split into
three phases (`scout`, `act`, `review`). Sonnet 4.5 handles scout
+ act; GPT-4.1 handles a final review pass. One bash call is
recorded as a retry (`retry_of` set).

Below are the three most useful report views, generated by
`report.py` against the sample log.

## `--by model` — total cost per model

```
| model | events | input | cache_read | hit_rate | output | cost_usd |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| claude-sonnet-4-5-20250929 | 9 | 204,932 | 160,800 | 78% | 5,460 | $0.28 |
| gpt-4.1 | 1 | 12,200 | 11,000 | 90% | 1,100 | $0.02 |

**Total cost (last 30 days):** $0.30
```

What this tells you: Sonnet did 93% of the work for 93% of the cost.
The cache hit rate is 78% on Sonnet, 90% on GPT-4.1. The GPT-4.1
hit rate is high because it ran a single review turn against an
already-warm prefix.

## `--by model,phase` — where each model spends its budget

```
| model | phase | events | input | cache_read | hit_rate | output | cost_usd |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| claude-sonnet-4-5-20250929 | act | 5 | 128,100 | 106,800 | 83% | 3,600 | $0.15 |
| claude-sonnet-4-5-20250929 | scout | 4 | 76,832 | 54,000 | 70% | 1,860 | $0.13 |
| gpt-4.1 | review | 1 | 12,200 | 11,000 | 90% | 1,100 | $0.02 |

**Total cost (last 30 days):** $0.30
```

What this tells you: scout has lower cache hit rate (70%) than act
(83%), even though scout reads less. The reason is the first scout
turn pays the cache-write cost for the long-lived context; later
turns benefit. If the mission becomes scout-heavy in production,
the hit rate gap will close.

## `--by phase,tool` — which tool calls are expensive

```
| phase | tool | events | input | cache_read | hit_rate | output | cost_usd |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| scout | (none) | 1 | 18,432 | 0 | 0% | 850 | $0.08 |
| act | (none) | 1 | 24,500 | 18,000 | 73% | 1,820 | $0.06 |
| act | edit | 2 | 50,700 | 44,400 | 88% | 1,160 | $0.05 |
| act | bash | 2 | 52,900 | 44,400 | 84% | 620 | $0.05 |
| scout | read_file | 2 | 38,600 | 36,000 | 93% | 800 | $0.03 |
| review | (none) | 1 | 12,200 | 11,000 | 90% | 1,100 | $0.02 |
| scout | grep | 1 | 19,800 | 18,000 | 91% | 210 | $0.01 |

**Total cost (last 30 days):** $0.30
```

What this tells you:

- The single most expensive event ($0.08) is the **first scout turn**
  — pure cache-write, 0% hit rate. Expected; this is the cost of
  warming the long-lived context.
- `act` "no-tool" turns (the agent's reasoning between tool calls)
  are the second-most expensive at $0.06. If you wanted to cut cost,
  shortening agent reasoning would matter more than dropping a tool.
- `bash` shows 2 events but the second one is the retry. The retry
  costs the same as the original. If your mission has a flaky tool,
  the retry overhead shows up here.

## What you'd act on

If this report were typical of your mission type:

1. **Scout warm-up cost is fundamental** — you pay it once per
   mission. If you run many short missions, batch them into one
   session to amortize.
2. **Reasoning-only turns dominate `act`** — if you want to cut
   cost, smaller models for the reasoning passes (with Sonnet
   reserved for the actual edits) is the highest-leverage move.
3. **The retry is invisible in the rolled-up totals** but visible
   in `--by phase,tool` because the count is 2 instead of 1. If
   retries become common, that's a tool-stability bug, not a cost
   bug — fix the bash flakiness.
